Introduction

Overview of the Mossi People

The Mossi people, also known as the Moose, are the largest ethnic group in Burkina Faso, comprising approximately half of the country's population. They primarily inhabit the central plateau region, including the capital city, Ouagadougou. The Mossi society is traditionally organized into a hierarchical system with a central authority figure known as the "Moro Naba" (king), who resides in Ouagadougou. This system of chieftaincy has been a unifying force among the Mossi for centuries.

Scope and Structure

Historically, Mossi society was organized into several kingdoms, the most prominent being the Yatenga Kingdom, Ouagadougou Kingdom, and Tenkodogo Kingdom. Each kingdom was ruled by a king (the Mogho Naba) who wielded considerable authority.


Clan Structure: Mossi society is organized into clans (koms) that trace their lineage through patrilineal descent. Each clan is led by a chief (nakomse).

Social Structure and Culture

The Mossi people of West Africa are organized into clans led by chiefs, tracing their lineage through patrilineal descent. Their culture thrives on oral traditions, including storytelling and music, while their religious practices blend animism with ancestor worship. Crafts such as pottery and weaving hold significant cultural value, reflecting their rich heritage and community cohesion.

2. Family and kinship Systems

The Mossi family and kinship system revolves around patrilineal descent, where lineage is traced through male ancestors. Extended families are integral, providing support and upholding traditions. Clans, led by chiefs (nakomse), organize individuals sharing common ancestry, fostering unity and mutual aid. Marriage is significant, often arranged to strengthen alliances between clans. Gender roles are defined, with men typically responsible for provision and protection, while women manage household affairs and childcare, maintaining familial harmony and continuity.

3. Cultural Practices and Traditions

Mossi culture thrives on oral traditions, animist beliefs, and vibrant arts. They celebrate through storytelling, music, and dance, honoring ancestors and fostering community bonds. Artisans excel in pottery, weaving, and woodcraft, preserving cultural identity. Festivals and ceremonies mark important occasions, showcasing traditional attire and cuisine, including millet-based dishes, reflecting their rich heritage and communal spirit.

Political Organization

Mossi political organization centers around kingdoms led by a paramount chief (Mogho Naba) and chiefs (nakomse) overseeing clans (koms). This hierarchical system historically governed local affairs and justice, adapting over time while preserving cultural identity and leadership roles within modern national contexts.

The role of the Mohgo Naba

The Mogho Naba, or king, holds supreme authority in Mossi society, overseeing political, judicial, and ceremonial duties while symbolizing unity and cultural continuity among the people.

Governance and administration

Mossi governance historically revolves around kingdoms led by a paramount chief, the Mogho Naba, who holds supreme authority. Each kingdom is further organized into clans led by chiefs (nakomse), who oversee local governance, justice, and community affairs. This hierarchical system ensures order and cohesion within Mossi society, balancing traditional leadership with modern influences.
