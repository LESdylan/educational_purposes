Introduction

Ladies and gentlemen, language enthusiasts, and grammar aficionados, welcome to our delightful dive into the depths of the English language! Today, we’re going to tackle a tense that’s as perfect as a well-brewed cup of coffee—yes, I’m talking about the Present Perfect!

Now, you might be wondering, what’s so special about the Present Perfect? Well, imagine it as the time machine of tenses—it connects the past with the present in a way that’s just...perfect! Have you ever felt like the past is still lingering around, just like that one sock that always goes missing in the laundry? That’s the Present Perfect for you. It’s the tense that’s been bringing the past into the present since...well, forever!

We'll explore how it works, why it’s used, and we might even uncover some perfect examples along the way. By the end of this lecture, you’ll have mastered the art of using the Present Perfect in ways that would make Shakespeare himself green with envy—or perhaps we should say, "you will have mastered," because by then, it will be something you’ve accomplished.

So, get ready to have your linguistic skills perfectly polished, and remember, if grammar had a Hall of Fame, the Present Perfect would have been inducted long ago! Let's dive in and see how the past has perfectly shaped our present language use.

present/perfect

Le présent parfait (Present Perfect) est un temps verbal en anglais qui est utilisé pour exprimer des actions ou des événements qui ont une relation avec le présent. C'est un temps qui lie le passé et le présent, souvent utilisé pour décrire des expériences, des actions qui ont des résultats dans le présent, ou des événements récents.

Syntax

Le présent parfait se forme avec l'auxiliaire "have" (ou "has" pour la troisième personne du singulier) suivi du participe passé du verbe principal.

  1. Sujet+have/has+participe passé

Exemples

  1. I have visited Paris. (J'ai visité Paris.)
  2. She has finished her homework. (Elle a fini ses devoirs.)

specifications

Ever
Have you ever been to London? (Es-tu déjà allé à Londres ?)
Never
used for experiences that have never been experienced.
Just
Used for actions very recent
Already
Used for any type of actions that had happened earlier than expected
Yet
Used in questions and negative sentences
